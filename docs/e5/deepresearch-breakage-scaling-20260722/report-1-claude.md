# How Often Do AI Coding Agents Break Working Code — and Does It Get Worse at Scale?

## TL;DR
- **AI coding agents break previously-working behavior at material and measurable rates**: instance-level regression rates (patches breaking ≥1 pre-existing test) cluster in the ~16–37% range on single-shot benchmarks (TensorBench: 16.1–36.7%; TDAD vanilla baseline: 30.2%), and roughly 29.6% of "solved" SWE-bench Verified patches diverge behaviorally from ground truth.
- **Breakage compounds along a sequence of changes**: dedicated continual/milestone benchmarks show sharp degradation — SWE-Milestone/EvoClaw drops from >80% (isolated) to ≤38% (continuous); SWE-CI finds most models have a zero-regression rate below 0.25 (regressions in 75%+ of maintenance tasks) and regression *frequency* rises with iteration count for 12 of 20 models; SlopCodeBench shows structural erosion rising in 80% of trajectories.
- **Breakage scales with codebase/patch size**: resolution collapses (and regression risk rises) as repositories grow — FeatBench reports resolved rates falling from ~60–70% (<200 files/<50k LOC) to 10–30% (>800 files/>300k LOC). But the specific axis (a) — does regression rate rise with the *number of pre-existing tests* per task — has never been directly measured with a correlation coefficient; it remains an open empirical gap.

## Key Findings

**The three scaling axes, current state of evidence:**
- **(a) Number of behaviors/tests that must stay true:** *Largely unmeasured directly.* No primary paper reports a correlation coefficient between the number of pre-existing (P2P) tests per instance and the probability of breaking one. Indirect evidence: catastrophic regressions concentrate in large-suite instances (astropy-13977 with 322 P2P tests broken entirely; django-13089 with 352). SWE-EVO explicitly links its larger suites (avg 874 tests/instance) and multi-file scope to "stronger regression risk," but stops short of a coefficient.
- **(b) Length of a sequence of changes:** *Best-measured axis.* Multiple 2026 continual/iterative benchmarks converge: performance drops sharply and quality erodes monotonically as agents build on their own prior work. Regression frequency rises with iteration count.
- **(c) Codebase/patch size & complexity:** *Well-measured for resolution, partly for regression.* Strong inverse correlation between repo size and success; regression risk explicitly rises with "aggressive implementation" and larger patches.

## Details

### 1. Single-shot P2P / regression rates (rigorously measured)

**TDAD: Test-Driven Agentic Development (arXiv:2603.17973, 2026).** On SWE-bench Verified (Qwen3-Coder 30B, 100 instances), the vanilla agent produced 562 P2P test failures — a 6.08% test-level regression rate (total P2P failures / total P2P tests) and an average of 6.5 broken tests per generated patch, with 3 "catastrophic" instances where all P2P tests failed. Instance-level regression rate (fraction of generated patches breaking ≥1 P2P test) was 30.2%. Their GraphRAG+TDD method cut test-level failures 72% (562→155) and the regression rate 70% (6.08%→1.82%). Notably, TDD prompting *without* localization made regressions worse (799 failures, 9.94%). This is the cleanest published decomposition of test-level vs. instance-level regression. URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.17973

**TensorBench (arXiv:2606.05570, 2026).** Reports the fraction of applied patches introducing ≥1 regression in the pre-existing suite (instance-level regression rate): Claude 4.7 = 16.1%, Claude 4.6 = 27.1%, Codex 5.5 = 23.6%, Codex 5.3 = 28.1%, Codex 5.4 = 36.7%. Key finding: "Pass rate and resistance to regressions do not always improve together within a single model family" — Codex 5.4's 36.7% break-rate is worse than both its predecessor and successor even though pass rate improved monotonically. URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05570

**"Are 'Solved Issues' in SWE-bench Really Solved Correctly?" (PatchDiff, arXiv:2503.15223, ICSE 2026).** Applied differential testing to plausible (test-passing) patches from three SOTA tools on SWE-bench Verified: 29.6% of plausible patches induce at least one behavioral divergence versus the human ground-truth patch. Manual inspection of a 30% sample found 28.6% "certainly incorrect," implying ~11.0% of all reported successes are invalid; combined weaknesses inflate reported resolution rates by 6.2 absolute percentage points. Also: 7.8% of patches count as correct while failing the developer-written suite. This measures behavioral drift/hidden regressions that P2P tests miss. URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.15223

**METR mergeability note (metr.org, March 2026).** In METR's "Many SWE-bench-Passing PRs Would Not Be Merged into Main" (Whitfill, Wu, Becker, Rush), 4 maintainers from 3 repos (scikit-learn, Sphinx, pytest) reviewed 296 AI-generated PRs that passed SWE-bench Verified's automated grader; maintainer merge decisions ran ~24 percentage points below the grader, and against a 68% golden-patch baseline the best model's adjusted acceptance was ~50% — i.e., "roughly half of test-passing SWE-bench Verified PRs written by mid-2024 to mid/late-2025 agents would not be merged into main by repo maintainers." This is direct evidence that benchmark pass rates overstate true correctness, partly due to subtle regressions the tests don't cover. URL: https://metr.org/notes/2026-03-10-many-swe-bench-passing-prs-would-not-be-merged-into-main/

**AgentLens (arXiv:2605.12925, 2026).** Of 1,136 passing SWE-bench Verified trajectories, 10.7% are "Lucky Passes" reached through behavior including regression cycles and blind retries; only 20.2% are "Ideal." Lucky rates range 0.5%–23.2% across eight model backends. URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.12925

### 2. Sequential / milestone / multi-session benchmarks (axis b — best evidence)

**SWE-CI (arXiv:2603.03823, 2026).** The most direct regression-over-time study: 100 tasks from 68 real Python repos, each spanning on average 233 days and 71 consecutive commits; 18–20 models, >10 billion tokens, max 20 CI iterations. Findings: most models achieve a zero-regression rate below 0.25 (i.e., they introduce a regression in 75%+ of tasks); only the two Claude-Opus models exceed 0.5. Crucially for compounding: computing Pearson correlation between iteration count and regression metrics, 12 of 20 models show a *positive* correlation between iterations and regression rate (regressions become more frequent as maintenance progresses), while 11 of 20 show a *negative* correlation for regression magnitude (each regression hits a shrinking share of the suite). Caveat: exact per-model decimals and r-values appear only in the paper's figures, not the text; and the measured correlation is with iteration count, not test-suite size. URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.03823

**SWE-Milestone / EvoClaw (arXiv:2603.13428, 2026).** Reconstructs verifiable "Milestone DAGs" from commit histories to test continuous evolution. Across 12 frontier models × 4 frameworks, overall scores drop from >80% on isolated tasks to at most 38.03% in continuous settings; even the strongest agents resolve only ~13% of milestones in full sequences. The paper attributes this explicitly to "a snowball effect of accumulating technical debt" and inability to prevent regressions. URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.13428

**SlopCodeBench (arXiv:2603.24755, 2026).** 20 problems, 93 checkpoints where agents extend their own prior code under evolving specs. Across 11 models, no agent solves any problem end-to-end; highest strict checkpoint solve rate (which includes earlier-checkpoint regression tests) is 17.2% (Opus 4.6), dropping to 0.5% by the final checkpoint. Structural erosion rises in 80% of trajectories, verbosity in 89.8%; agent code is 2.2× more verbose than maintained human repos, and human code quality stays flat over time while agent code deteriorates monotonically. Quality-focused prompts cut initial erosion ~30% but do not slow the *rate* of degradation. Deng et al. (2026), cited within, preserved agent code across 98 milestones over 7 repos and saw pass rates drop from 80% in isolation to 38% under continuity. URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24755

**EvoCode-Bench (arXiv:2605.24110, 2026).** 26 multi-round tasks, 227 evaluated rounds (5–15 rounds each) in persistent multi-turn sessions where requirements evolve and conflict. Explicitly designed to detect "when the evolving workspace first stops satisfying the active specification," materializing decisions into schemas/APIs that "constrain later work and can create regressions." URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.24110

**SWE-EVO (arXiv:2512.18470, 2026).** 48 release-sized tasks (avg 21 files, avg 874 tests/instance). GPT-5.2 drops from 72.80% on SWE-bench Verified to 22.92% on SWE-EVO. Strict scoring: breaking any P2P test zeroes the whole instance. Larger, multi-file, multi-PR instances associated with "stronger regression risk." URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.18470

**SWE-INTERACT (arXiv:2606.30573, 2026)** and related multi-turn testbeds convert single-shot benchmarks into interactive developer workflows, documenting capability gaps when requirements are revealed progressively.

### 3. Codebase-size scaling (axis c — well-measured for resolution)

**FeatBench (arXiv:2509.22237, 2026).** Strong inverse correlation between repository complexity and resolved rate: agents reach 60–70% (GPT-5) on small repos (<200 files or <50k LOC) but converge to 10–30% on large repos (>800 files or >300k LOC). Patch complexity: success peaks for single-file, 1–30 LOC patches (~36% top rate) and falls to nearly zero for patches >50 LOC or spanning 5+ files. Regression is a named failure mode: all agents "frequently introduce regressions, breaking existing functionality when adding new features," often due to "aggressive implementation" (over-extending beyond the request). URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.22237

**"Evaluating Software Development Agents" (arXiv:2410.12468, 2024/2025).** 4,892 patches from 10 top agents on 500 SWE-bench Verified issues: resolved issues involved significantly fewer/less complex files, lower cyclomatic complexity, and smaller gold patches (mean 9.29 vs. 24.12 total lines changed). Notably a partial NULL result on regression: "Most agents maintained code reliability and security, avoiding new bugs or vulnerabilities; while some agents increased code complexity, many reduced code duplication and code smells." URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.12468

**MobileDev-Bench (arXiv:2603.24946, 2026).** Patch size strongly predicts success: ≤10-line patches resolve at 14.3–16.1% (automated retrieval), >500-line patches at 0.0%; median patch for resolved instances 7.0 lines vs. 106.0 for unresolved (15.1× difference). URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24946

### 4. Industry telemetry & code-quality longitudinal reports (measured, but not pure regression)

**GitClear AI Copilot Code Quality reports (2024/2025/2026).** Analyzed 211M changed lines (2020–2024, incl. Google/Microsoft/Meta repos). Findings: code churn (lines reverted/revised within two weeks) "grew from 3.1% in 2020 to 5.7% in 2024," projected to "double in 2024 compared to its 2021, pre-AI baseline"; "moved" (refactored) code "decreased dramatically from 24.1% in 2020 to just 9.5% in 2024" (a 44% drop from the previous year), making 2024 "the first year on record where within-commit copy/paste exceeded 'moved' (refactored) code"; copy/pasted lines "rose from 8.3% to 12.3%" (2021–2024). In 2024 "the frequency of duplicated code blocks increased eightfold," roughly 10× the level of two years prior. GitClear's 2026 "Maintainability Gap" report extends the trend: copy/paste "climbed from 9.4% (in 2022) to 15.7% in the first half of 2026" (developers now ~5× more likely to duplicate than refactor), and block duplication rose from 40.3 per million changed lines (2023) to 73.0 (2026 YTD). These are quality/maintainability signals (churn, duplication) — proxies for downstream defect/regression risk, not direct P2P measurements. Flag: GitClear is a vendor with a commercial interest, though the dataset is large and the methodology is documented. URL: https://www.gitclear.com/ai_assistant_code_quality_2025_research

**Cognition/Devin 2025 Performance Review.** Reports ~67% of Devin PRs merged vs. ~34% previously; a customer (Litera) saw "regression cycles 93% faster." These are marketing/vendor self-reported figures, not independent regression measurements — treat as claims, not rigorous evidence. URL: https://cognition.ai/blog/devin-annual-performance-review-2025

### 5. Analogous degradation-with-scale (grounding the scaling shape only)

**METR task-horizon research (Kwa et al., arXiv:2503.14499, 2025).** Success probability declines with task length: near-100% on tasks taking humans <~4 minutes, <10% on tasks taking humans >~4 hours; task length is highly correlated with success (R²=0.83). Frontier models such as Claude 3.7 Sonnet have a 50% time horizon of around 50 minutes, and the frontier time horizon has been "doubling approximately every seven months since 2019, though the trend may have accelerated in 2024" (the 80% horizon has essentially the same doubling time — 213 vs. 212 days). Establishes the general shape — longer horizon → lower reliability — that underlies compounding regressions. URL: https://metr.org/blog/2025-03-19-measuring-ai-ability-to-complete-long-tasks/

**"Lost in the Middle" (Liu et al., TACL 2024, arXiv:2307.03172).** U-shaped accuracy curve: performance degrades significantly when relevant info sits in the middle of a long context — a mechanism for why agents forget earlier constraints/behaviors as context grows. DOI: 10.1162/tacl_a_00638

**Instruction-following under multiple constraints.** SWE-IF (arXiv:2510.07315) shows task-level success collapses under 3+ simultaneous instructions with "exponential decay" as each added constraint multiplies failure probability. SEQUOR (arXiv:2605.06353) shows constraint adherence degrades substantially as turns increase and constraints accumulate. These support axis (a)'s expected *shape* (more simultaneous constraints → multiplicatively lower joint satisfaction) even though coding-specific P2P-vs-test-count data is missing.

### 6. NULL / negative results

- **arXiv:2410.12468** (above): most agents avoided introducing new bugs or security vulnerabilities and often reduced duplication/code smells — a partial null on regression at the single-shot level.
- **SWE-CI**: the two Claude-Opus models exceed a 0.5 zero-regression rate — evidence that low regression is achievable for the strongest models even in continual settings.
- **Aider Polyglot** (225 Exercism edit tasks with a second-attempt-after-test-feedback loop) measures edit correctness/format compliance but is a from-scratch exercise benchmark with no pre-existing behavior to preserve — hence not a regression measurement, and excluded as such.

## Recommendations

1. **Treat single-shot benchmark "resolve rates" as an upper bound on real reliability.** Discount by the measured regression/behavioral-divergence overhead: assume ~16–37% of applied patches break ≥1 pre-existing test (TensorBench/TDAD) and ~30% of "solved" patches diverge behaviorally from intent (PatchDiff). For merge-readiness, expect roughly half of benchmark-passing PRs to fail human review (METR: ~50% adjusted acceptance against a 68% golden-patch baseline).
2. **For multi-session / long-horizon agentic work, budget explicitly for compounding regressions.** The measured cliff is severe: >80% → ≤38% from isolated to continuous (SWE-Milestone), and 75%+ of maintenance tasks incur a regression for most models (SWE-CI). Mitigations with measured effect: run pre-existing regression suites after every change (P2P filtering raised effective correctness in SWE-Fixer; GraphRAG+TDD cut regression rate 70% in TDAD), prefer localized/surgical patches, and split large tasks into smaller sub-tasks (arXiv:2410.12468).
3. **Gate on codebase size.** Reliability data supports restricting fully-autonomous agent work to smaller/simpler modules (<200 files, <50k LOC, single-file patches <30 LOC), where resolved rates are 60–70% vs. 10–30% for large repos. Above those thresholds, keep a human in the loop and require CI green on the full pre-existing suite before merge.
4. **Adopt the strongest models where regression cost is high.** Only Claude-Opus-class models exceeded a 0.5 zero-regression rate in SWE-CI; per-model differences are large and non-monotonic with pass rate (TensorBench), so choose on regression resistance, not headline resolve rate.
5. **Instrument your own telemetry.** Track churn, revert rates, and P2P failures over time (GitClear-style) as leading indicators; the benchmarks show quality erosion is monotonic and largely invisible to pass/fail metrics.

**Benchmarks that would change these recommendations:** if a model achieves a zero-regression rate >0.5 on SWE-CI *and* holds checkpoint-strict solve rate flat (not decaying) on SlopCodeBench, autonomous multi-session work becomes defensible. If large-repo (>800 files) resolved rates rise above ~50%, the codebase-size gate can be relaxed.

## Caveats

- **Axis (a) is essentially unmeasured.** No study directly quantifies whether regression probability rises with the *number* of pre-existing tests/requirements per task via a correlation coefficient. The evidence is indirect (catastrophic regressions cluster in large-suite instances; instruction-following decays multiplicatively with constraint count). This is the single biggest open empirical gap.
- **The continual-coding benchmarks are very new (all 2026) and small.** SWE-CI = 100 tasks, all Python/open-source; SlopCodeBench = 20 problems; EvoCode-Bench = 26 tasks; SWE-EVO = 48 tasks. Enterprise codebases with proprietary deps and mixed stacks are unrepresented. Exact per-model numbers in SWE-CI live only in figures.
- **Date anomaly / provenance flag.** Many primary sources carry 2026 arXiv IDs and reference 2026-era model names (GPT-5.x, Claude Opus 4.x); treat as recent preprints, some not yet peer-reviewed. ICSE 2026 (PatchDiff) and TACL 2024 (Lost in the Middle) are peer-reviewed; most benchmark papers are preprints.
- **Vendor sources (GitClear, Cognition/Devin) have commercial interests** and are flagged accordingly; GitClear's data is large and methodologically documented (quality proxies, not direct regression), while Devin's merge-rate and "regression cycle" figures are self-reported marketing claims.
- **Benchmark resolve rates are contaminated/inflated** in ways that cut against optimism (PatchDiff's 6.2pp inflation; METR's ~50% merge-rate haircut), so the *true* regression picture is likely somewhat worse than headline numbers suggest.

## Synthesis (5 lines)
1. **Axis (b) — sequence length — is the best-measured and shows the clearest, steepest degradation:** isolated→continuous scores collapse >80%→≤38% (SWE-Milestone), 75%+ of maintenance tasks incur regressions (SWE-CI), regression *frequency* rises with iteration count for 12/20 models, and code quality erodes monotonically (SlopCodeBench: erosion up in 80%, verbosity in 89.8% of trajectories).
2. **Axis (c) — codebase/patch size — shows a strong, consistent inverse relationship for resolution (60–70%→10–30% as repos grow past ~800 files/300k LOC)** with regression explicitly named as a size-linked failure mode, though regression rate itself is not cleanly plotted against LOC.
3. **On single changes, baseline breakage is already substantial:** ~16–37% of patches break a pre-existing test and ~30% of "solved" patches silently diverge from intended behavior.
4. **Axis (a) — regression rate vs. the number of pre-existing tests/requirements — has essentially never been directly measured;** the shape is only inferable from adjacent evidence (catastrophic regressions in large-suite instances; multiplicative instruction-following decay).
5. **Open gaps:** no correlation coefficient for axis (a); almost no data on enterprise/proprietary/multi-language codebases; per-model regression decimals often buried in figures; and no longitudinal, independent (non-vendor) telemetry tying accumulating AI code to measured production regression rates over time.
