# No Data, Just Inference: The Unmeasured Relationship Between Scale and AI-Coding Agent-Induced Regression

## Quantitative Evidence on Regression Accumulation in Multi-Turn Tasks

The accumulation of regressions over the course of a complex, multi-turn development task represents one of the most significant challenges for AI coding agents. Unlike single-shot code generation, where the primary metric is often whether a given task is completed correctly, agentic workflows involve a sequence of actions that can build upon and interact with each other over time [[90]]. This temporal dimension introduces a critical failure mode: a change made at an early stage of the interaction can inadvertently break functionality that was previously working, and this broken state can then propagate through subsequent steps, making it difficult to diagnose the root cause [[3]]. While numerous sources discuss this problem qualitatively, there is a notable scarcity of rigorously measured, quantitative data detailing how this regression rate scales with task length. The available evidence suggests that even a low probability of error at any given turn can lead to a high likelihood of observable regression by the end of a long task, due to cascading failures [[3]]. The measurement methodologies for detecting such pass-to-pass violations are well-established in principle; they involve instrumenting CI/CD pipelines to parse exit codes, monitoring telemetry signals for test status changes, and using traceability tools to correlate state modifications with test outcomes [[44,95,97]]. However, the systematic application of these methods across varying task horizons remains largely unexplored in the provided literature.

The most direct and compelling quantitative finding on this topic comes from the preliminary study "Regression Accumulation in Multi-Turn LLM Programming" [[93]]. This research provides a crucial, albeit isolated, data point on the final outcome of multi-turn interactions. Across six different language models evaluated on programming tasks, the study found that a significant portion of tasks—ranging from 40% to 73%—experienced a loss of previously correct behavior by the end of the interaction [[93]]. A regression is formally defined as a code change causing a previously passing test to fail, a phenomenon particularly damaging in long-term software maintenance [[94]]. This finding establishes a powerful upper bound on the potential for regression in agentic workflows. It quantifies the severity of the problem, showing that for a substantial fraction of tasks, the agent's interventions ultimately result in breaking existing functionality. However, this single data point is insufficient to characterize the functional form of the relationship between task length and regression rate. The study does not report on the *rate* of accumulation; it is unknown whether regressions happen gradually throughout the process or occur in large bursts. Furthermore, the research did not systematically vary the length of the tasks to determine if a longer horizon leads to a higher percentage of regression. The reported figures represent a snapshot of the final state, not a time-series of degradation. Therefore, while it confirms that regression is a major issue, it cannot be used to model how the risk grows with task duration. This highlights a critical gap in the literature, where qualitative concerns about long-horizon coherence and reliability are prevalent, but concrete, scalable metrics are absent [[99,120]].

Beyond this single quantitative study, the broader body of research offers strong qualitative evidence supporting the hypothesis that regression rates increase with task length. The concept of "agent lifespan engineering" has been proposed, which shifts the focus from day-one benchmark scores to whether an agent remains dependable over time [[99]]. This philosophical shift implicitly acknowledges that regression is a temporal problem. Similarly, research into agentic crafting emphasizes the need for LLMs to operate effectively over multiple turns in real-world environments, a challenge complicated by the lack of persistent memory [[90,120]]. Agents forget project conventions and repeat known mistakes, creating a fertile ground for regressions to accumulate during long, multi-session projects [[120]]. Field studies also indicate that developers engage in naturalistic, multi-session deployments of AI tools, making longitudinal studies essential for understanding sustained user engagement and trust [[2]]. Yet, no publicly available field telemetry from vendors like GitHub or Amazon provides granular data on regression rates broken down by session count or project age. Vendor reports focus on high-level productivity gains and security fixes, offering no insight into the intermediate step of behavioral breakage [[14,15,72]]. This reliance on anecdotal evidence and vendor claims, rather than measured telemetry, leaves a significant void in our understanding of regression in real-world usage scenarios. The few existing benchmarks that touch upon this area, such as SWE-EVO for long-horizon evaluation and SlopCodeBench which notes that a mistake can zero out later checkpoints, reinforce the conceptual understanding of cumulative risk but do not provide the necessary numerical data to quantify it [[109,113]].

In summary, the investigation into how regression scales with the length of development tasks yields one powerful quantitative data point indicating high final-state failure rates, but nothing that allows for modeling the growth of this risk over time. The theoretical foundation for expecting a positive correlation is strong, based on concepts like cascading failures and the inherent limitations of current agentic memory systems [[3,120]]. However, this remains a hypothesis grounded in qualitative analysis and inference, not in empirically validated data. The field has yet to produce controlled experiments or public telemetry studies that systematically measure pass-to-pass violations as a function of task horizon or session count.

## The Impact of Behavioral Constraints on Agent-Induced Breakage

A central challenge in AI-assisted software engineering is ensuring that newly generated code adheres to all existing constraints and requirements. This axis of inquiry focuses on how the rate of regression—a previously passing test failing after an agent's intervention—is affected by the number of established behaviors that must be preserved. These behaviors are typically encoded in unit tests, integration tests, formal specifications, or documented system requirements. While the literature consistently identifies preserving existing functionality as a primary goal, there is a profound lack of empirical, quantitative research that measures how the regression rate changes as the number of these behavioral invariants increases. The provided sources contain no studies reporting a regression rate for a scenario with ten required behaviors versus one with a thousand. This represents a complete knowledge vacuum on the specified first scaling axis. The absence of such data means that the relationship between constraint density and agent-induced breakage is purely speculative, despite its obvious importance for safety-critical and legacy systems.

The available evidence points towards a strong qualitative expectation that more constraints lead to a higher risk of regression, but this is inferred from the nature of the problem itself rather than from direct measurement. Benchmarks designed to evaluate coding agents are beginning to incorporate this reality. For instance, SlopCodeBench explicitly recognizes that regression tests serve to carry forward earlier requirements, meaning that a single mistake introduced early in a sequence of changes can invalidate progress on later checkpoints [[109]]. This design choice implies an understanding that as the number of checkpoints (and thus established behaviors) grows, so too does the potential for cumulative regression to derail a task. Similarly, benchmarks like SWE-CI define a regression as a code change that causes a previously passing test to fail, and they use this as a core evaluation criterion, requiring that a "correct patch should not introduce regression failures" in already-passing tests [[61,113]]. The very existence of these benchmarks underscores the centrality of the problem. However, their results typically aggregate performance across tasks and do not disaggregate success or failure rates based on the number of pre-existing tests or constraints per task. This represents a missed opportunity to gather incidental data that could help characterize the relationship. Even within a single task, the complexity of managing multiple simultaneous instructions or constraints can degrade LLM performance, suggesting that as the number of independent behavioral rules increases, the agent's ability to satisfy them all without introducing unintended side effects diminishes [[9,89]]. While this is not a direct measurement of regression against a growing set of *past* behaviors, it serves as a plausible proxy, indicating that constraint management is a bottleneck for current models.

Furthermore, the concept of "specification drift" or requirements drift in AI-assisted development workflows is identified as a relevant research area in the initial query . This refers to a situation where the agent's output diverges from the original intent or specification over the course of a multi-step refinement process. Such drift would inevitably manifest as a regression if the original specification is represented by a set of passing tests. However, no empirical studies on this specific phenomenon were found in the provided materials. Research on agentic reasoning has shown that semi-formal reasoning can help catch omissions that would otherwise break existing tests, highlighting the value of maintaining awareness of past states [[121]]. Yet, without systematic measurement, the extent to which specification drift contributes to overall regression rates remains unknown. The lack of tools or methodologies specifically designed to track and quantify specification drift further compounds this knowledge gap. The focus in much of the literature remains on achieving a final solution that passes all tests, often overlooking the integrity of the path taken to get there and the preservation of all intermediate behavioral guarantees. This focus on ultimate task success, rather than on the preservation of all existing properties, is a fundamental limitation in the current landscape of empirical evaluation. It means that benchmarks may report a task as successful even if the agent broke dozens of previously passing tests along the way, as long as the final output passes a new, comprehensive suite of tests. This practice fails to capture the true cost of agent-induced breakage in terms of developer verification effort and systemic fragility [[40]]. Without explicit instrumentation to log the status of every test before and after each agent action, it is impossible to accurately compute a pass-to-pass violation rate, let alone study how it scales with the number of tests.

## Regression Risk in Large-Scale and Long-Horizon Codebases

The third scaling axis investigates how the size and complexity of the underlying codebase influence the rate at which AI coding agents induce regressions. This is a critical question for practical adoption, as many organizations rely on large, complex, and often legacy codebases where the risk of breaking existing functionality is inherently high. The provided sources reveal a clear pattern: while there is strong qualitative reasoning and indirect evidence linking codebase characteristics to regression risk, there is a complete absence of empirical, quantitative data that directly measures this relationship. No study in the provided context links the lines of code or number of files in a repository to a numerically reported regression rate caused by AI agents. This stands in stark contrast to the wealth of information available on agent performance on small-scale, self-contained coding problems. The challenge lies in moving beyond these simplified environments to the real-world complexity of large-scale software evolution [[25]].

Several sources establish a connection between codebase characteristics and software quality issues, which can be seen as precursors to regression. One study documented that increases in codebase size are a major determinant of increased static analysis warnings and overall code complexity, absorbing most of the variance in code quality metrics [[23]]. This finding establishes a correlation between size and inherent system fragility. A larger, more complex codebase is statistically more likely to have hidden dependencies and fragile subsystems, making it a more challenging environment for an agent to navigate without causing unintended consequences. However, this study does not connect this inherent fragility to the specific act of AI-assisted modification. Another plausible mechanism is the limitation of LLM context windows. As codebases grow, the amount of relevant context that can be fed to the model at any one time becomes constrained [[127]]. An agent operating on a large codebase may lose access to crucial information about functions, types, or business logic located outside its immediate context window, increasing the likelihood of generating code that breaks something elsewhere in the system [[98]]. This is a widely accepted hypothesis in the field, but again, it lacks empirical validation from the provided sources. The performance of LLMs has been shown to degrade when context length is artificially extended, even if the model can theoretically process the information perfectly, suggesting that simply providing more context may not be a panacea [[127]].

Repository-level benchmarks exist that acknowledge the diversity of codebase sizes, providing a platform for future research. For example, Rust-SWE-bench demonstrates significant variation, with its projects averaging nearly 1000 files and over 128,000 lines of code [[63]]. Other benchmarks like PROJDEVBENCH are designed to evaluate agents on entire repositories based on project-level requirements [[28]]. These benchmarks are a step in the right direction, as they move beyond simple function-level completions. However, the abstracts and descriptions provided do not contain disaggregated results that would allow for analyzing regression rates as a function of codebase size. This represents a significant missed opportunity to generate incidental data on the specified axis. The use of agents for modernizing legacy applications further underscores the importance of this question, as this use case inherently involves navigating and modifying large, complex systems [[75]]. While anecdotal reports suggest that agents can accelerate this process [[65]], there are no quantitative studies measuring the corresponding regression rates in these real-world scenarios. The lack of such data prevents a nuanced understanding of how agent capabilities and risks evolve as the scale of the target software changes. Without controlled studies comparing agent performance across a range of codebases—from small libraries to massive enterprise monoliths—it is impossible to determine if the relationship is linear, super-linear, or if there is a threshold effect where regressions become unavoidable above a certain size. This remains one of the most significant unmeasured dimensions in the evaluation of AI coding agents.

## Synthesis of Measured Relationships and Unexplored Dimensions

The comprehensive review of the provided research reveals a critical disparity between the recognized importance of regression in AI-assisted software development and the availability of rigorous, quantitative data to measure its scaling properties. While the problem is frequently cited as a primary failure mode for coding agents, empirical evidence that directly addresses the three specified axes—number of established behaviors, length of development tasks, and codebase size—is exceptionally scarce [[61,94,108]]. The research landscape is characterized by a heavy emphasis on overall task success rates and a relative neglect of behavioral integrity, where the preservation of previously working code is paramount. This focus on the final outcome over the journey means that the intermediate, and often costly, process of regression accumulation is frequently overlooked in standard evaluations. The following synthesis consolidates the findings, distinguishes between measured relationships and qualitative trends, and explicitly identifies the dimensions that remain entirely unmeasured.

The only direct, quantitative evidence available pertains to the second scaling axis: the length of development tasks. The study "Regression Accumulation in Multi-Turn LLM Programming" provides a crucial data point, showing that between 40% and 73% of tasks resulted in a loss of previously correct behavior after a full multi-turn interaction [[93]]. This finding robustly confirms that regression is a pervasive and significant issue in agentic workflows. However, this single data point is insufficient to characterize the functional form of the breakage-vs-task-length relationship. It represents a final-state outcome, not a time-series of degradation. We cannot determine if regressions accumulate linearly, exponentially, or in bursts. Consequently, the shape of the relationship remains unknown. For the other two axes, no quantitative data exists in the provided sources. There is no measured data on how regression rates scale with the number of established behaviors (axis a) or the size/complexity of the codebase (axis c). The insights for these axes are therefore purely qualitative and inferential. For axis (a), the expectation is that more constraints increase risk, supported by benchmark designs that treat regression tests as carrying forward requirements [[109]]. For axis (c), the expectation is also that larger, more complex codebases present a higher risk, supported by findings that larger codebases have more inherent complexity and warnings [[23]], combined with the known limitations of LLM context windows [[127]].

This requires instrumenting development environments to log the status of every test before and after each agent intervention, enabling a fine-grained analysis of behavioral integrity throughout the entire development lifecycle. Without this level of empirical rigor, our understanding of AI coding agents will remain skewed towards optimistic task completion metrics, obscuring the very real and costly problem of regression.
